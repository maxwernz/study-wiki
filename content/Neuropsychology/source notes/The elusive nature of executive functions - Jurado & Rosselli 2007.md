# The elusive nature of executive functions (Jurado & Rosselli 2007)

## TL;DR

A review arguing that "executive function" still **lacks a formal definition**: many
overlapping subcomponents and contradictory findings. It traces the construct's history,
neural substrates, and its development-to-decline trajectory across the lifespan.

## Topic context

The conceptual backbone for [[Executive Functions]] — where the definitional problem, the
historical roots (Baddeley & Hitch's central executive; Lezak's four components; Luria's
frontal "programming") and the lifespan story come from.

## Problem / question

What *are* executive functions, why is the literature so inconsistent, and how do they change
over the lifespan?

## Approach / method

Narrative review of definitions, measurement problems, proposed neural substrates, and
lifespan changes, focusing on four abilities: attentional control, planning, set-shifting,
and verbal fluency.

## Key findings

- **No agreed definition.** EF is invoked constantly yet "awaits a formal definition"; many
  proposed subcomponents (see their Table 1).
- **History:** "central executive" (Baddeley & Hitch 1974); Lezak's four components
  (goal formation, planning, carrying out plans, effective performance); Luria located it in
  frontal lobes.
- **Lifespan:** EF develops with frontal myelination — inhibition of overlearned behaviour
  emerges **first** in children, verbal fluency **last**; in ageing, inhibition of irrelevant
  information declines earlier than set-shifting and fluency.
- **Methodological caveat:** results generalise poorly because tasks and definitions differ
  across studies (task impurity).
