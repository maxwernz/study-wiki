# Object Orientation Agnosia & Pusher Syndrome — Lecture Slides

## Summary

This deck covers two distinct disorders that both reveal how the brain represents
**spatial properties of objects and the body**.

**Part 1 — Object orientation agnosia.** Some patients can perfectly **identify** an
object (a face, a letter, an animal) regardless of how it is rotated, yet **cannot say
which way up it is**. Identity and orientation dissociate. The deck frames this with two
families of object-recognition theory — **viewpoint-dependent** (recognise by mentally
rotating the image to a canonical view) and **viewpoint-invariant** (recognise from an
object-centred description independent of view) — and uses the dissociation as evidence
that recognition can proceed **without viewer-centred (orientation) information**.

**Part 2 — Pusher syndrome.** Stroke patients who **actively push** with their non-paretic
arm/leg toward the paralysed (contralesional) side and **resist correction** of their tilt.
The cause is a **misperception of body orientation relative to gravity**: they feel
"upright" when actually tilted ~20° to the ipsilesional side. Strikingly, their perception
of the *visual* vertical is normal — so this is a disturbance of a **second, body-based
graviceptive system**, separate from the visual-vestibular one. Lesion: posterolateral
**thalamus** (also insula/postcentral gyrus).

## Key points

**Object orientation agnosia**
- Classic case **Z** (Friedrich Best, 1917; Ferber & Karnath 2003): recognised stimuli
  even upside down but could not judge their orientation; bilateral parieto-occipital
  lesions (precuneus/angular gyrus).
- Patient **KB** (Karnath et al., Neuropsychologia 2000): Bálint-Holmes syndrome
  (simultanagnosia, optic ataxia, spatial disorientation), no neglect/field defect;
  identified letters/objects/animals/faces at any orientation but failed to judge
  orientation when non-upright (−90°, +90°, 180°). Near-chance for non-upright, normal at 0°.
- **Viewpoint-dependent** theory: find major axis → rotate to vertical → scale → match to
  template (the "A" example). **Viewpoint-invariant** theory: object-centred description
  about a principal axis (the vase example), indifferent to viewpoint.
- **Perrett et al. (1998):** objects are coded by **neurons tuned to view, orientation and
  size**; more neurons are tuned to **frequently-experienced (upright)** orientations — so
  upright knowledge is more robust to damage. This explains why these patients keep upright
  knowledge but lose non-upright orientation judgement (not a *general* orientation agnosia).

**Pusher syndrome**
- **Contraversive pushing** (Davies 1985): uses non-paretic limbs to push toward the
  hemiparetic (contralesional) side; resists passive correction of the tilt.
- **Altered perception of body orientation to gravity:** with eyes closed, patients set
  their body "upright" when tilted ~18–20° to the ipsilesional side (Karnath et al.,
  Neurology 2000).
- **Double dissociation (SVV vs SPV):** Pusher patients have **normal Subjective Visual
  Vertical (SVV)** but **tilted Subjective Postural Vertical (SPV)**. Vestibular-disorder
  patients show the **opposite** (tilted SVV, normal SPV). → two separate systems.
- **Second graviceptive system:** a trunk-based gravity sense, possibly from **truncal
  graviceptors** (inertia of abdominal mass/blood; Mittelstaedt 1992/1998), processed in
  posterior thalamus/insula/postcentral gyrus — separate from the visual-vestibular system.
- **Lesion:** posterolateral **thalamus** (VPL/VPM, lateral/ventral posterior nuclei;
  Karnath et al. PNAS 2000, Neurology 2005), in both left and right hemispheres.

## Methods, models, or theories

- **Viewpoint-dependent vs viewpoint-invariant object recognition** (see
  [[Object Orientation Agnosia]]).
- **Forced-choice orientation judgement** at 0°/±90°/180° (KB) to separate identity from
  orientation and upright from non-upright.
- **SVV / SPV adjustment tasks** (luminous rod in darkness; lateral tilt chair) — see
  [[Pusher Syndrome]].

## Equations or formal definitions

None (qualitative). The key quantitative anchor: SPV tilt of **~18–20° ipsilesional** in
pushers, vs near-zero SVV tilt.

## Local relevance

Covers two "What should I know" topics: **object orientation agnosia** and **pusher
syndrome**. Object orientation agnosia ties into the broader **dual visual streams** /
visual-agnosia material; pusher syndrome contrasts directly with [[Spatial Neglect]]
(roll-plane vs yaw-plane body-orientation disturbances).

name the thalamic lesion.
