# The return of Phineas Gage (Damasio et al. 1994)

## TL;DR

Reconstructing Gage's lesion from his **skull** + neuroimaging shows the damage was
**bilateral ventromedial prefrontal cortex**, sparing motor/language areas — the exact profile
that, in modern patients, impairs **rational decision-making and emotion processing**.

## Topic context

The modern bookend to [[Recovery from the passage of an iron bar through the head - Harlow 1868]];
it ties the 19th-century case to a specific, replicable **vmPFC syndrome**. Core to
[[Frontal Lobe Syndrome and Phineas Gage]] and [[Value-Based Decision Making and the vmPFC]].

## Problem / question

No autopsy was performed; the lesion's exact location (and whether it was left-only or
bilateral) was unknown. Where, in the "vast frontal territory," was Gage actually damaged?

## Approach / method

Measured Gage's surviving skull; used the known iron dimensions and the two fixed entry/exit
holes to enumerate physically possible trajectories; warped a normal brain into the skull and
intersected it with the most likely trajectory to estimate the lesion (early 3-D
neuroimaging-based reconstruction).

## Key findings

- The lesion involved **both left and right prefrontal cortices**, concentrated in the
  **ventromedial** region — **not** the left-only damage long assumed.
- **Motor and language cortices were spared**, explaining intact movement and speech.
- This lesion pattern matches Damasio's modern vmPFC patients, who show a **defect in rational
  decision making and the processing of emotion** (somatic-marker framework).

## Limitations / open questions

The reconstruction is a **probabilistic estimate** from skull geometry, not a verified lesion;
later re-analyses (e.g. Van Horn 2012) emphasise white-matter disconnection too.
