# Pusher Syndrome — a frequent but little-known disturbance of body orientation perception (Karnath, J Neurol 2007)

## TL;DR

The comprehensive review of pusher syndrome. Pushers **misperceive their body's orientation
in the coronal (roll) plane** — they feel upright when tilted ~20° to the ipsilesional side
— and actively push toward the paralysed side, resisting correction. Their **visual**
vertical is normal, so this is a **second, body-based graviceptive system** (separate from
visual-vestibular), localised to the **posterolateral thalamus** (± insula/postcentral
gyrus). It dissociates anatomically and functionally from [[Spatial Neglect]].

## Key findings

- **Definition:** uses the **non-paretic** arm/leg to **push toward the contralesional
  (hemiparetic) side** ("contraversive pushing") and **resists** passive correction.
  ~10% of acute hemiparetic strokes. Easily missed because patients are examined supine —
  must be **sat or stood up** to see it; physios/nurses often detect it first.
- **Not just old labels.** Differs from: **listing phenomenon** (patient *knows* they are
  falling and clings on, falls to the *paretic* side); **lateropulsion** (Wallenberg's,
  falls *ipsiversively*, no active pushing); **thalamic astasia** (similar lesion but
  studied in patients with little/no weakness — may dissociate).
- **Not caused by neglect.** Correlates with neglect after right lesions and with **aphasia**
  after left lesions, because the cortical pusher area sits near both — but pushing occurs in
  ~20% of right pushers *without* neglect and in left-lesion pushers who have aphasia not
  neglect. Roughly 60% right / 40% left hemisphere.
- **The double dissociation (SVV vs SPV):** pushers have **normal Subjective Visual Vertical
  (SVV)** but a **~18–20° ipsilesional tilt of Subjective Postural Vertical (SPV)**.
  Vestibular-disorder patients show the **opposite** (tilted SVV, normal SPV). → two separate
  systems for sensing the visual world's vs the body's verticality.
- **Second graviceptive system.** A non-visual, trunk-based gravity sense. Candidate inputs:
  **truncal graviceptors** (inertia of abdominal viscera/blood; Mittelstaedt) and/or
  somatosensory input — though somatosensory loss alone does **not** cause pushing (a patient
  with complete hemibody sensory loss had normal SPV). Processed in **posterolateral
  thalamus, insula, postcentral gyrus** (VPL/VPM thalamocortical projections).
- **Counter-intuitive direction:** SPV is tilted **ipsilesionally**, yet pushing is
  **contraversive**. Proposed reasons: the patient pushes to compensate the visual-vs-postural
  mismatch (pushing eases with eyes closed), or pushing is a reaction to losing balance when
  they move toward their (ipsilesionally-shifted) felt-upright. The **non-paretic leg** is
  constantly tilted ipsiversively — a specific clinical sign (Johannsen et al. 2006).
- **Roll vs yaw:** pusher syndrome = body-orientation disturbance in the **coronal/roll**
  plane; spatial neglect = disturbance in the **transverse/yaw** plane → **separate neural
  subsystems for body orientation in different spatial dimensions** (camptocormia is a
  possible pitch-plane analogue, pathogenesis unclear).
- **Prognosis:** transient — recovers in most patients within weeks to ~6 months.
