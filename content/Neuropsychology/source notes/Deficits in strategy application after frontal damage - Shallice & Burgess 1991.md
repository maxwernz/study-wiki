# Deficits in strategy application after frontal lobe damage (Shallice & Burgess 1991)

## TL;DR

Frontal patients who score **normally on standard tests** (IQ, perception, language, even
"frontal" tests) fail at **open-ended, multi-goal, self-organised** tasks — because they
cannot **reactivate previously formed intentions** that the environment is not cueing.

## Topic context

The classic demonstration of the **bedside-test paradox** in [[Executive Functions]] and
[[Frontal Lobe Syndrome and Phineas Gage]]: why preserved test scores can coexist with
disastrous real-world functioning.

## Problem / question

Why do some frontal patients with severe everyday-life disorganisation pass quantitative
neuropsychological tests? What deficit do standard tests miss?

## Approach / method

Three patients with traumatic prefrontal injuries; two novel **open-ended, multi-subgoal**
tasks (the Six Elements Test and a real-world Multiple Errands Test) requiring self-scheduling
over 15–30 minutes with minimal external structure. Memory and motivation explanations were
explicitly excluded.

## Key findings

- All three were impaired on the **multiple-subgoal tasks** despite good perception, language,
  IQ, and (for two) good performance on other frontal tests.
- Characteristic failure: spending **too long on individual tasks**, not switching, breaking
  rules — i.e. failing to **plan and manage multiple goals over time**.
- Interpretation: an inability to **reactivate delayed intentions** when not directly signalled
  by the stimulus situation — standard tests give a single explicit cued problem, so they don't
  expose this.

## Limitations / open questions

Three patients; lesions heterogeneous (traumatic). The "strategy application" / intention-
reactivation account is one of several interpretations of frontal disorganisation.
