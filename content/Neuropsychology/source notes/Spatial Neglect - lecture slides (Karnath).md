# Spatial Neglect — Lecture Slides (Karnath)

## Summary

This is the core lecture deck for the Spatial neglect topic (Karnath's own slides).
It builds one central argument: spatial neglect after right-hemisphere stroke is, at
its heart, **not** a pure "attention" deficit but a disturbance of the brain's
**egocentric (body-centred) representation of space**. The whole reference frame that
the brain uses to locate objects relative to the body is rotated to a new "default
position" on the ipsilesional (right) side, so the patient's exploration, gaze, and
attention are all biased rightward and the left side is neglected.

The deck moves through: (1) the clinical picture — patients deviate eyes and head to
the right even when "doing nothing" in darkness; (2) the anatomy — a right
**perisylvian network** (temporal, parietal, frontal cortex plus connecting white
matter); (3) animal/causal evidence (superior colliculus inactivation, superior
temporal sulcus in macaque); and (4) the mechanism — the **transformation hypothesis**:
visual space must be transformed from retinotopic → head-centred → body-centred
coordinates, and in neglect this transformation carries a systematic error. The
clinching evidence is that manipulating the sensory inputs that feed this
transformation (**caloric vestibular stimulation, neck-muscle vibration**) temporarily
shifts the bias back toward the midline and even abolishes neglect.

## Key points

- **Core sign:** a deviation of exploratory movements (eyes, head, hand) toward the
  ipsilesional right; the centre of exploration no longer matches the body's sagittal
  mid-plane but is shifted right (slides 8, 18).
- The bias is present **even at rest in complete darkness** ("doing nothing"), so it
  cannot be explained by external stimuli capturing attention (slide 1; cf. the at-rest
  scan paths).
- **Anatomy:** a right perisylvian network for spatial attention/orientation —
  middle/superior temporal gyri, inferior parietal lobule, inferior/middle frontal
  gyri, insula, putamen, caudate nucleus, plus white-matter tracts (SLF, uncinate, IOF,
  ILF, SOF) (slides 4–5).
- **Causal animal evidence:** reversibly inactivating the **superior colliculus** in
  macaques induces neglect-like attentional deficits; the strongest fMRI modulation loss
  sat in a small region on the floor of the **superior temporal sulcus** (Bogadhi et al.,
  Curr Biol 2019; slides 6–7).
- **No gravity-based reference:** when patients are tilted (left/right/back/forward),
  the bias does not track gravity — it tracks the **body** (egocentric), not the world
  (Karnath et al., J Cogn Neurosci 1998; slides 9–12).
- **Mechanism — transformation hypothesis:** to know where an object is relative to the
  body, the brain combines retinal input with eye-in-head, head-in-space and
  head-on-trunk signals; sources include **eye-muscle proprioception, efference copy,
  vestibular input, neck-muscle proprioception** (slide 14). In neglect this integration
  works with a systematic error → the egocentric frame is rotated rightward (slide 13, 18).
- **Sensory modulation proves the point:** caloric vestibular stimulation (Rubens 1985)
  and posterior neck-muscle vibration (Karnath et al. Brain 1993, EBR 1995/1996)
  transiently move the exploration centre back toward the trunk midline and reduce/abolish
  neglect; the same stimulation induces neglect-*like* behaviour in healthy people
  (slides 15–17).

## Methods, models, or theories

- **Transformation hypothesis (Karnath 1994):** a model of how egocentric space is
  built (see [[Egocentric Reference Frame and Coordinate Transformation]]). Inputs:
  retinotopic image + eye/head/trunk position signals. Output: a body-centred map used
  for exploration and orienting. Neglect = a systematic error in this transformation.
- **Visual-search recordings:** combined eye+head movements recorded while patients
  search a surrounding array; the *distribution* of exploration is the read-out of where
  spatial attention is allocated.
- **Sensory-stimulation paradigm:** caloric (cold-water ear irrigation) and neck-muscle
  vibration as causal manipulations of the egocentric frame.

## Equations or formal definitions

No formal equations in this deck. The key "formal" idea is the **coordinate-transformation
chain** (qualitative):

$$\text{retinotopic} \xrightarrow{\text{eye-in-head}} \text{head-centred}
\xrightarrow{\text{head-on-trunk + vestibular}} \text{body-centred (egocentric)}$$

Read in words: the position of an object on the retina is successively re-referenced —
first to the head (using where the eye points in the head), then to the trunk (using
where the head sits on the trunk, signalled by neck proprioception and the vestibular
organ) — to yield a stable body-centred location used for action. Each arrow is a place
where an error can enter; in neglect the net result is a rightward-rotated frame.

## Local relevance

This deck is the spine of the Spatial neglect topic and the source for the three
"What should I know" points: **definition & clinical symptoms, gross anatomy, underlying
mechanisms/models**. The papers in the same folder elaborate each: the 2015 review
([[Spatial attention systems in spatial neglect - Karnath 2015]]) on mechanism, the 2012
