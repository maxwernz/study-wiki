# Executive Dysfunction (Rabinovici et al. 2015)

## TL;DR

A clinically oriented review: EF splits into **four components — working memory, inhibition,
set shifting, fluency** — supported not just by PFC but by a **distributed network**
(parietal, basal ganglia, thalamus, cerebellum, white matter, neuromodulators). Hence
executive dysfunction is pervasive across neurological disease.

## Topic context

The clean clinical framework used in [[Executive Functions]]: the four-component scheme and
the "EF is a distributed network, not just the frontal lobe" correction.

## Problem / question

How should clinicians classify, localise, diagnose and treat disorders of executive function?

## Approach / method

Clinical review: components of EF, bedside/neuropsychological tests for each, neuroanatomic
correlates, and management.

## Key findings

- **Four components:** working memory, inhibition, set shifting, fluency — which combine into
  higher constructs (planning, organisation) and **dissociate** across patients.
- **Distributed substrate:** intact EF needs PFC **plus** parietal cortex, basal ganglia,
  thalamus, cerebellum, their white-matter links, and cholinergic/noradrenergic/serotonergic/
  dopaminergic systems. Damage anywhere → dysexecutive symptoms.
- **Clinical ubiquity:** executive dysfunction appears in most neurodegenerative diseases and
  many neurologic/psychiatric/systemic illnesses; very common in practice.
- **Management:** treat the underlying cause + occupational therapy / rehab to maximise
  function and safety.
