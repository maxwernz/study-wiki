# Spatial attention systems in spatial neglect (Karnath, Neuropsychologia 2015)

## TL;DR

Neglect has classically been read as a disorder of *spatial attention*. Karnath argues
this needs adjustment: in neglect the **stimulus-driven (bottom-up) attention system is
disturbed but the goal-directed (top-down) system is preserved**. The famous rightward
bias does **not** come from a broken top-down attention; it comes from an **altered
body-related (egocentric) reference frame** on top of which attention operates.

## Topic context

Spatial attention is implemented by two cortical networks (Corbetta & Shulman): a
**dorsal** goal-directed network (intraparietal sulcus, frontal eye fields) and a
**ventral** stimulus-driven network (ventral frontal cortex, temporo-parietal junction).
Neglect follows predominantly right-hemisphere lesions that hit the **ventral** network.
This paper tests the leading "interhemispheric imbalance" account against patients' actual
exploration behaviour.

## Method / argument structure

A conceptual review marshalling behavioural eye-movement studies. The decisive test:
the **interhemispheric imbalance model** (Kinsbourne; Corbetta et al. 2005) predicts a
continuous left→right *gradient* of attention, so patients should keep turning further
right ("circling"). Eye-movement data contradict this.

## Key findings

- **Against pure imbalance:** when patients explore a scene that surrounds them, the
  distribution of fixations is **bell-shaped** with a peak shifted right (~+7° to +13°),
  then *decreases* toward more eccentric right positions — not a monotonic gradient to
  the far right edge. The peak is far from the anatomical limits of eye/head movement
  (Karnath et al. 1998).
- **Imbalance of BOLD ≠ behaviour:** the interhemispheric fMRI push–pull pattern also
  appears in non-neglect stroke patients and even on non-spatial tasks; it scales with
  distance from the lesion, so it need not reflect the behavioural deficit.
- **Dissociation of the two attention systems (Niemeier & Karnath 2000, 2003):** within
  the spontaneously explored region, top-down (voluntary) saccades show **no
  direction-specific deficit** (all directions equally good); but **stimulus-driven**
  saccades to the same locations show a direction-specific leftward deficit. → disturbed
  bottom-up, preserved top-down.
- **The bias is a frame shift:** the bell-shaped exploration is centred on a "new"
  equilibrium rotated rightward — an **altered egocentric reference frame**, not a broken
  attention system. This fits the ventral-network lesion site.
- **ISO-map model:** object-based and egocentric coding interact — object-centred neglect
  eases when objects sit at more ipsilesional egocentric positions (Karnath et al. 2011).
