# Recovery from the passage of an iron bar through the head (Harlow 1868)

## TL;DR

The founding clinical case of neuropsychology: Phineas Gage survived an iron bar through his
frontal lobe with intact body, speech and memory but a transformed **personality** —
"Gage was no longer Gage." First evidence that the frontal lobe governs character and
self-regulation.

## Topic context

The historical anchor for [[Frontal Lobe Syndrome and Phineas Gage]] and the whole
[[Executive Functions]] lecture. Harlow's report is the **20-years-later sequel** to his
original 1848 account, written to put the case on permanent record.

## Problem / question

Could a man survive such massive frontal destruction — and if so, what does the aftermath
reveal about what the frontal lobes *do*?

## Approach / method

A physician's longitudinal case report: the accident, the acute survival and wound, the
physical recovery, and (the immortal part) the long-term **behavioural/personality change**,
traced through Gage's wandering post-injury life.

## Key findings

- **The accident (13 Sept 1848):** a tamping iron (~3 ft 7 in, ~1¼ in diameter, ~13¼ lb) was
  blasted point-first through Gage's left cheek and out the top of the frontal bone.
- **Astonishing acute survival:** conscious within minutes, talking and walking; survived ~12
  more years.
- **Preserved:** strength, movement, speech, memory, "intelligence in the conventional sense."
- **Destroyed:** the balance "between his intellectual faculties and animal propensities" —
  he became fitful, irreverent, profane, unable to honour commitments or stick to plans;
  dismissed by employers who had called him their most efficient man.

## Limitations / open questions

A single case, no autopsy, lesion location unknown in 1868 (resolved only in 1994). Reporting
of the personality change is qualitative and partly second-hand.
