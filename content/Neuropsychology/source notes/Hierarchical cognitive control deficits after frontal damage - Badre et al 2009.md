# Hierarchical cognitive control deficits after frontal damage (Badre et al. 2009)

## TL;DR

The **lesion test** of the rostro-caudal hierarchy: **rostral** frontal lesions impair
**abstract** control, **caudal** lesions impair **concrete** control, and damage at a level
impairs that level **and all more abstract levels above it** — direct causal evidence for a
hierarchical frontal organisation.

## Topic context

Supplies the **necessity** evidence behind [[Hierarchical Cognitive Control]], complementing the
correlational imaging of
[[The architecture of cognitive control in the PFC - Koechlin et al 2003]] and
[[Is the rostro-caudal axis of the frontal lobe hierarchical - Badre & D'Esposito 2009]].

## Problem / question

Imaging suggests a rostro-caudal hierarchy, but is it **causally necessary**, and does it behave
like a true **processing** hierarchy (lesions affecting their level + higher levels)?

## Approach / method

Frontal-lesion patients performed four tasks requiring control at **increasing levels of
abstraction** (1st- to higher-order response selection); deficits were related to **lesion
location** along the rostro-caudal axis.

## Key findings

- **Rostral lesions → deficits on more abstract tasks; caudal lesions → deficits on more
  concrete tasks** (a double dissociation along the axis).
- Damage at a given level impaired that level **and** the more abstract levels above it, but
  spared more concrete levels — the signature of a **processing hierarchy**.
- Two adjacent regions dissociated by **level of control**, matching prior fMRI.

## Limitations / open questions

Human lesions are heterogeneous and rarely confined to one rostro-caudal band; level boundaries
are task-defined.
