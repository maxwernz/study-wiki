# Friedrich Best's Case Z with misidentification of object orientation (Ferber & Karnath, *Classic Cases in Neuropsychology* Vol. II, 2003)

## TL;DR

The historical founding case of **object orientation agnosia**. In 1917 the ophthalmologist
**Friedrich Best** described a soldier ("Z", case 38) with a penetrating brain injury who
could **recognise objects even upside down but could not judge their orientation** — a clean
dissociation between **object identity** and **object orientation**. A modern reanalysis
(KB, Karnath et al. 2000) refines it: upright knowledge is **preserved**, so it is not a
*general* orientation agnosia.

## Topic context

The classic-case anchor for the object-orientation-agnosia lecture; pairs with the
theoretical [[The neuropsychology of object constancy - Turnbull et al 1997]] review.

## Method / argument structure

Historical case description + reanalysis. Best presented figures/letters/portraits (e.g. the
German emperor) upright or rotated 180° and tested identity vs orientation judgement.

## Key findings

- **Patient Z:** recognised any stimulus regardless of orientation, but **could not determine
  its orientation** — could not tell a hand's pointing direction, or "n" from "u". Training
  did not help. He also had **simultanagnosia, optic ataxia, loss of egocentric visual
  localization, and exploratory-eye-movement deficits** → a **Bálint(-Holmes) syndrome**.
- **Lesion (autopsy):** bilateral parietal — left angular gyrus/precuneus and right
  precuneus/posterior inferior parietal. Best localised the orientation "centre" to the
  **precuneus / superior parietal lobule** (medial surface).
- **Best's interpretation:** the brain can recognise objects **without viewer-centred
  information** — orientation processing is "sharply separated" from identity processing.
- **Six later cases** show the same dissociation (WB, RM, LG, NL, SC, KB).
- **Karnath et al. (2000) refinement (patient KB):** with a forced-choice 0°/±90°/180° test,
  **upright orientation knowledge was perfectly preserved**; only **non-upright** orientation
  judgement failed. So these patients are **not** generally agnosic for orientation. This fits
  **Perrett et al.'s (1998)** view-tuned-neuron account: more neurons code the frequently-seen
  **upright** view, so upright knowledge is more robust to damage / recovers earlier — and no
  dedicated "orientation system" need be lost.
