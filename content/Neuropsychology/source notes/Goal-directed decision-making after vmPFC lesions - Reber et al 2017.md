# Goal-directed decision-making after vmPFC lesions (Reber et al. 2017)

## TL;DR

Using **outcome devaluation**, vmPFC-lesion patients keep choosing a food devalued by satiation
— a **selective goal-directed deficit** — while their **habitual learning** and **hedonic
ratings** stay intact. First proof that human vmPFC is **necessary** for goal-directed choice.

## Topic context

Establishes necessity (not just correlation) for [[Value-Based Decision Making and the vmPFC]];
imports the animal goal-directed/habitual framework into human lesion work.

## Problem / question

Neuroimaging implicates vmPFC in goal-directed behaviour, but is vmPFC **necessary** for it?
Can the goal-directed and habitual systems be dissociated in humans by lesion?

## Approach / method

**Outcome devaluation** (canonical animal test): participants learn to work for food outcomes,
then one outcome is **devalued by selective satiation**; goal-directed control = stopping work
for the devalued outcome. Compared vmPFC-lesion patients to brain-damaged comparison patients.

## Key findings

- vmPFC patients **persistently chose the devalued outcome** → impaired **goal-directed**
  control.
- They showed **intact acquisition of instrumental contingencies** → **habitual** control
  preserved.
- They gave **normal hedonic ratings** of the devalued food → experienced value intact.
- Conclusion: vmPFC is **necessary and selective** for goal-directed choice, reconciling human
  data with animal lesion studies and explaining real-world vmPFC decision deficits.

## Limitations / open questions

Lesions are naturally occurring and variable; devaluation isolates one facet of goal-directed
control. Doesn't localise within vmPFC.
