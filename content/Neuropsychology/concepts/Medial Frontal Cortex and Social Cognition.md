# Medial Frontal Cortex and Social Cognition

## Short definition

The **medial frontal cortex (MFC)** — the inner wall of the frontal lobe, including anterior
cingulate and frontopolar regions — is central to **social cognition**: thinking about other
minds, oneself, and the value of social outcomes. Frontal/medial damage can impair
**empathy** and **theory of mind**, a distinctly social side of executive dysfunction.

## Intuition

Most of executive function is about controlling *your own* behaviour. But humans live in a
social world, where success depends on modelling **other people's** goals, feelings and likely
actions — "what is she thinking? will he be hurt? is this fair?" The MFC is the brain's
**social modelling room**. Like the rest of the frontal lobe it doesn't *see* faces or *hear*
words (that's posterior cortex); it builds the **internal model of minds** — yours and
others' — that guides social behaviour. Lose it and you can still talk and reason, but you
stop reading the room.

## Explanation

**MFC is a hub of the social-cognition network (Amodio & Frith 2006).** Social cognition —
representing internal states, knowledge of self, perception of others, interpersonal motives —
engages a network: **MFC** (they fold the ACC into MFC), **temporoparietal junction (TPJ)**,
**superior temporal sulcus (STS)**, and **temporal poles**. Within it the MFC plays a
**special, integrative** role while the posterior nodes do more specific jobs. Amodio & Frith
organise MFC along a **posterior→anterior (caudal→rostral) gradient** that mirrors the
abstraction gradient of [[Hierarchical Cognitive Control]]:

- **Posterior MFC (≈ dACC region)** — action monitoring, outcome/conflict monitoring (the
  link to [[Conflict Monitoring and the Anterior Cingulate Cortex]]).
- **Orbital / ventral MFC** — monitoring the **value** of outcomes, including social/emotional
  value (link to [[Value-Based Decision Making and the vmPFC]]).
- **Anterior rostral MFC (frontopolar, ~BA 10/9)** — the most abstract, "**meta**" social
  processing: representing **mental states** — others' and one's own — i.e. mentalising /
  theory of mind, comparing self to others.

So social cognition isn't bolted on; it reuses the **same medial machinery** (monitoring →
value → abstract representation) that the EF lecture builds elsewhere, applied to **minds and
social outcomes**.

**Lesions reveal what is *necessary* (Hillis 2014).** Functional imaging shows where social
processing *correlates* with activity, but only lesions show which areas are **necessary**.
Hillis reviews focal-lesion (mainly stroke) evidence on **empathy** and distinguishes two
dissociable systems (Shamay-Tsoory's model):

- an evolutionarily/developmentally **early "emotional contagion"** system — sharing/catching
  another's feelings — leaning on **inferior frontal / ventral** and limbic circuitry;
- a **later "cognitive perspective-taking"** system — *inferring* what another thinks/feels —
  leaning on **(ventro)medial prefrontal cortex** and the mentalising network.

These can be **doubly dissociated**: a patient can lose the ability to *share* emotions while
keeping the ability to *infer* them, or vice versa, depending on lesion site. This grounds
empathy in identifiable frontal circuitry and explains the **social/emotional bluntness** of
vmPFC patients — the affective, interpersonal face of frontal-lobe syndrome
([[Frontal Lobe Syndrome and Phineas Gage]]).

## Worked example — reading the MFC map

![[amodio-frith-mfc-anatomy.png]]
*Amodio & Frith (2006), Fig. 1: medial view of the frontal lobe with Brodmann areas plotted in
a Talairach grid — posterior/dorsal action-monitoring regions (e.g. BA 8/24/32, the cingulate)
through anterior frontopolar mentalising cortex (BA 9/10) and ventral/orbital value regions
(BA 14/25). Social processing is laid out along the same caudal→rostral abstraction gradient
as cognitive control.*

Concretely: judging **"did my action cause that bad outcome?"** engages posterior MFC
(monitoring); judging **"how much do I value pleasing this person?"** engages orbital MFC
(value); judging **"does my friend *believe* the box is empty even though I know it isn't?"**
(a false-belief / theory-of-mind problem) engages anterior frontopolar MFC.

## Role in this class or project

This is the **social/emotional** wing of the Executive Functions lecture — the reason frontal
syndrome includes *interpersonal* breakdown, not just cold cognitive deficits. It ties the
lecture's medial-frontal threads together: monitoring
([[Conflict Monitoring and the Anterior Cingulate Cortex]]), value
([[Value-Based Decision Making and the vmPFC]]), and abstraction
([[Hierarchical Cognitive Control]]) reappear here, applied to minds.

## Exam, assignment, or project relevance

Know: the **social-cognition network** (MFC + TPJ + STS + temporal poles) with **MFC as
integrative hub**; the **caudal→rostral MFC gradient** (monitoring → value → mentalising);
the **two-system model of empathy** (emotional contagion vs cognitive perspective-taking) and
that lesion studies **dissociate** them; and **why functional imaging needs lesion evidence**
(correlation vs necessity — Hillis's central methodological point).

## Common confusions

- **"Empathy is one thing."** It splits into **emotional sharing** vs **cognitive inference**,
  with different neural substrates that dissociate after lesions.
- **Imaging ≠ necessity.** A region lighting up in an empathy task may not be required for it;
  Hillis's whole point is that lesions are needed to establish necessity.
- **MFC ≠ only ACC.** Amodio & Frith use "MFC" broadly to span cingulate, orbital and
  frontopolar medial cortex — a gradient, not a single area.
- **Social deficits are part of frontal syndrome**, not a separate disorder — the same medial
  machinery underlies them.
