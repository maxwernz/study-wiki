# Executive Functions

## Short definition

Executive functions (EF) are the high-level control processes that organise, coordinate,
and steer simpler cognitive operations toward a goal — they are the brain's "management"
layer, not the workers themselves.

## Intuition

Think of an orchestra. The individual musicians (memory, perception, language, motor
control) can each play perfectly well on their own. The **conductor** does not play any
instrument; the conductor decides which section comes in when, holds the score in mind,
hushes the brass when they drown out the strings, and adapts if a player goes off-script.
Executive functions are the conductor. This is why a patient can keep a high IQ, intact
memory, and fluent speech, yet still fail catastrophically at *running their own life*:
the players are fine, but the conductor is gone. The classic illustration is the
Eslinger–Damasio accountant (reported in [[Deficits in strategy application after frontal damage - Shallice & Burgess 1991]])
who scored IQ >130 after orbitofrontal surgery but could not decide which restaurant to
go to, went bankrupt, and lost job after job.

## Explanation

The term has always been **slippery** — Jurado & Rosselli open their review by noting that
despite constant use, EF "still awaits a formal definition." It was born from two roots:
Baddeley & Hitch's (1974) **"central executive"** (the attentional controller of working
memory) and Lezak's (1983) clinical definition of EF as the dimension of behaviour
concerned with **how** behaviour is expressed — its four pieces being **goal formation,
planning, carrying out goal-directed plans, and effective performance**. Luria had earlier
located this organising capacity in the frontal lobes, calling them the apparatus for
"programming, regulating and verifying" activity.

Modern clinical practice (Rabinovici et al. 2015) breaks EF into **four separable
components**:

1. **Working memory** — holding and manipulating information online (see [[Working Memory and the Prefrontal Cortex]]).
2. **Inhibition** — suppressing irrelevant or prepotent (habitual) responses.
3. **Set shifting** — flexibly switching between tasks, rules, or mental sets.
4. **Fluency** — generating responses (words, designs) freely under a rule.

These combine to support higher-order constructs such as **planning, organisation,
self-monitoring, and abstraction**. Crucially the components **dissociate**: a patient can
be impaired on inhibition but fine on fluency, which is why EF is treated as a family of
related abilities rather than one thing.

A central, exam-relevant subtlety is the **distinction between routine and non-routine
action**. Well-practised, environment-triggered behaviour runs automatically and needs no
executive control. EF is recruited precisely when behaviour is **novel, under-specified,
requires overriding a habit, or must be coordinated across time** — exactly the situations
that standard neuropsychological tests (single explicit problem, examiner-structured) fail
to probe, which is why frontal patients can pass the tests and fail at life (Shallice &
Burgess's "strategy application" insight).

**Anatomy and dysexecutive syndrome.** EF is mediated *primarily* by the **prefrontal
cortex (PFC)**, and the historical evidence came from frontal-lobe patients ([[Frontal Lobe Syndrome and Phineas Gage]]).
But — a key modern correction — EF is **not** "the frontal lobe function." Intact EF relies
on **distributed networks**: PFC plus parietal cortex, basal ganglia, thalamus, and
cerebellum, their white-matter connections, and the dopaminergic / noradrenergic /
cholinergic / serotonergic neuromodulatory systems. Consequently **executive *dysfunction*
is one of the most common syndromes in clinical neurology**, appearing in stroke, traumatic
brain injury, and essentially every neurodegenerative disease — not just focal frontal
damage. Different sub-regions specialise: lateral PFC for working memory and cognitive
control, **vmPFC/orbitofrontal** for value-based decision and social/emotional regulation
([[Value-Based Decision Making and the vmPFC]]), **dorsal ACC** for monitoring and control
allocation ([[Conflict Monitoring and the Anterior Cingulate Cortex]]).

**Development and decline.** EF has a long developmental trajectory that mirrors frontal
myelination and synaptogenesis: inhibition of overlearned behaviour appears first in
children; verbal fluency last. In ageing the order of decline is roughly the reverse for
some functions — inhibition of irrelevant information declines earlier than set-shifting
and fluency — tracking frontal-lobe atrophy.

## Worked example — Stroop and the Tower of London

- **Stroop task:** name the **ink colour** of the word "RED" printed in blue. The habitual
  response (read the word) conflicts with the goal (name the colour). Succeeding needs
  **inhibition** + working-memory maintenance of the rule + conflict resolution. The conflict
  is registered by dACC, which recruits DLPFC control (see [[Conflict Monitoring and the Anterior Cingulate Cortex]]).
- **Tower of London / planning:** rearrange discs to a target configuration in the fewest
  moves. You must hold the goal state, simulate sub-moves, sequence them, and inhibit the
  tempting-but-wrong immediate move. Frontal patients characteristically make impulsive
  first moves and fail to plan ahead — the planning/goal-management failure Shallice &
  Burgess captured with open-ended "multiple errands" tasks.

## Formal definition / equations

EF is a cognitive construct, not a formal model, so there is no defining equation. The most
formal account in this lecture is the **Expected Value of Control** (Shenhav et al. 2013),
which casts one executive decision — *how much control to allocate* — as maximising
$\mathrm{EVC} = (\text{expected payoff of control}) - (\text{cognitive effort cost})$.
That is unpacked in [[Conflict Monitoring and the Anterior Cingulate Cortex]].

## Role in this class or project

This is the **umbrella concept** for the "Executive Functions and Dysfunction" lecture.
Everything else hangs off it: the historical frontal-lobe cases, working memory, the
hierarchical organisation of control, conflict monitoring, value-based decision, and social
cognition are all either **components of EF** or **specific mechanisms / sub-region accounts**
of how the frontal lobe implements it.

## Exam, assignment, or project relevance

Likely exam targets: the **four-component scheme** (working memory, inhibition, set shifting,
fluency); the **definitional problem**; **why EF ≠ "frontal lobe" but a distributed network**;
the **routine vs non-routine** distinction and why frontal patients pass bedside tests yet
fail in daily life; and the standard tests (Stroop, Wisconsin Card Sorting, Tower of London,
verbal fluency, Trail Making, multiple-errands).

## Common confusions

- **"EF lives in the frontal lobe."** EF *depends on* the frontal lobe but is implemented by
  distributed networks; damage anywhere in them (parietal, basal ganglia, thalamus,
  cerebellum, white matter) produces dysexecutive symptoms.
- **"EF is one ability."** It is a family of dissociable components — a patient can fail
  inhibition while passing fluency.
- **"Normal IQ / memory rules out EF problems."** The opposite is common: the hallmark
  frontal patient has preserved IQ and memory but disastrous real-world self-regulation.
- **Don't conflate the *central executive* (a working-memory subsystem) with the whole of
  EF** — the central executive is one historical root, not the entire construct.
