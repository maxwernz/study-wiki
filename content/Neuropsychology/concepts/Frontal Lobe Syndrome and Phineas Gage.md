# Frontal Lobe Syndrome and Phineas Gage

## Short definition

Damage to the prefrontal cortex — especially its **ventromedial / orbitofrontal** part — can
leave intelligence, memory, perception and language intact while devastating **personality,
social judgement, emotion and decision-making**. The historical anchor is **Phineas Gage**.

## Intuition

Imagine a car whose engine, wheels and steering all work perfectly, but the *driver* has
stopped caring where the car goes, ignores red lights, and can't be trusted to keep an
appointment. Nothing is mechanically broken — the problem is in the part that sets goals and
respects consequences. Frontal-lobe syndrome is damage to that "driver." Gage survived an
iron bar through his frontal lobe, kept his strength, speech and memory, yet — as everyone
who knew him put it — **"Gage was no longer Gage."**

## Explanation

**The case (Harlow 1868).** On 13 September 1848, 25-year-old railway foreman Phineas Gage
was tamping blasting powder when a premature explosion drove a **~3 cm-thick, ~109 cm-long,
~6 kg tamping iron** point-first through his left cheek, behind the left eye, and out through
the top of the frontal bone, landing many yards away. Astonishingly he was barely stunned,
spoke and walked within minutes, and survived another **12 years**. His physician John Harlow
documented both the recovery and the part that made the case immortal: the **change in
character**. Pre-injury he was responsible, capable, "a favourite". Post-injury he was
"fitful, irreverent, profane," could not stick to plans, and had lost the balance "between
his intellectual faculties and animal propensities."

**Why it mattered for science.** Gage is the founding case for the idea that the frontal
lobes are not "silent" association cortex but the seat of **personality, social conduct, and
the regulation of behaviour** — i.e. of [[Executive Functions]]. He launched the whole
neuropsychology of the frontal lobe.

**The modern reconstruction (Damasio et al. 1994).** No autopsy was done, but Gage's skull
survives. Hanna Damasio's team measured the skull, combined it with neuroimaging and the
known iron dimensions, and **reconstructed the probable trajectory and lesion**. The key
result: the damage was **not confined to the left**, as long assumed, but involved **both
left and right ventromedial / anterior prefrontal cortices**, while largely **sparing the
dorsolateral surface and motor/language areas**. This pattern — bilateral vmPFC damage — is
exactly the profile that, in Damasio's modern patients, causes a **defect in rational
decision-making and the processing of emotion** (the "somatic marker" line of work). So the
reconstruction tied the 19th-century anecdote to a specific, replicable
**vmPFC syndrome**.

**The vmPFC profile vs the dorsolateral profile.** This is the central dissociation of the
lecture:

- **Ventromedial / orbitofrontal damage** → preserved IQ, memory, and standard test
  performance, but **poor real-world judgement, impulsivity, blunted/altered emotion,
  inconsistent preferences, social inappropriateness**. Decision-making is the core casualty
  (see [[Value-Based Decision Making and the vmPFC]]).
- **Dorsolateral prefrontal damage** → more "cognitive" deficits: **working memory,
  planning, set-shifting, fluency** (see [[Working Memory and the Prefrontal Cortex]],
  [[Hierarchical Cognitive Control]]).

**The bedside-test paradox (Shallice & Burgess 1991).** Frontal (especially vmPFC) patients
notoriously **pass formal neuropsychological tests** — IQ, perception, language, even many
"frontal" tests — because those tests give a single, examiner-structured problem. Their
deficit only surfaces in **open-ended, multi-goal, self-organised** situations: the
Eslinger–Damasio accountant with IQ >130 who still went bankrupt; Shallice & Burgess's
patients who, given several simple errands to do over 15–30 minutes with no external
structure, dwelled too long on one task and failed to switch — an inability to **reactivate
previously formed intentions** that the environment was not cueing.

## Worked example — reading the skull reconstruction

![[gage-skull-rod-reconstruction-damasio1994.png]]
*Damasio et al. (1994), Fig. 4: the reconstructed 3-D skull with candidate iron trajectories.
The chosen path (V) enters under the left zygomatic arch and exits at the frontal midline; the
modelled lesion involves bilateral ventromedial prefrontal cortex while sparing motor and
language cortex — explaining intact movement/speech alongside ruined judgement.*

Read it as a forward model: fix the **two fixed points** the iron must pass (entry hole below
the cheekbone, exit hole at the top of the skull), enumerate the physically possible straight
rods through both (red = non-viable, those that would have killed him or hit incompatible
structures; the viable vector V in blue), then intersect the surviving trajectory with a
normal brain warped to Gage's skull. The intersection *is* the lesion estimate — bilateral
vmPFC.

## Role in this class or project

Provides the **historical and anatomical foundation** for the whole EF lecture: it is where
the link "prefrontal cortex ↔ executive/social/emotional control" was first drawn, and it
introduces the vmPFC-vs-dorsolateral dissociation that organises the later mechanism pages.

## Exam, assignment, or project relevance

Be ready to: narrate the Gage case and **what it demonstrated**; state what Damasio's 1994
reconstruction **added** (bilateral vmPFC, link to decision/emotion); contrast the **vmPFC vs
dorsolateral** frontal syndromes; and explain the **bedside-test paradox** (why these
patients look normal on tests).

## Common confusions

- **"Gage's lesion was on the left."** The 1994 reconstruction argues it was **bilateral
  vmPFC**; the old left-only claim is outdated.
- **"He recovered fully."** Physically yes; behaviourally no — the personality change was the
  whole point.
- **"Frontal damage lowers IQ."** Classic vmPFC cases keep high IQ and memory; that
  preservation is diagnostic, not reassuring.
- **Don't treat "frontal lobe syndrome" as one thing** — vmPFC (social/emotional/decision)
  and dorsolateral (working memory/planning) syndromes are distinct.
