# Spatial Neglect

## Short definition

Spatial neglect is a disorder, most often after **right-hemisphere stroke**, in which the
patient fails to orient toward, report, or act on people and objects on the
**contralesional (usually left)** side of space — not because they are blind or paralysed
there, but because their internal sense of "where things are relative to my body" is
shifted toward the ipsilesional (right) side.

## Intuition

Imagine your sense of "straight ahead" got quietly rotated about 20° to the right, and you
had no idea it had moved. You would naturally look right, search right, eat from the right
half of your plate, draw only the right half of a clock — and you would *not* feel that
anything was wrong, because your whole frame of reference moved with you. That rotated,
unnoticed "default position" is the essence of neglect. It is not that the left side is
dark; it is that the left side has fallen outside the part of space your brain currently
treats as "in front of me."

## Explanation

**Clinical picture.** Acute right-hemisphere stroke patients spontaneously deviate their
eyes and head toward the right, *even when doing nothing in a dark room* — so the bias is
not caused by something on the right grabbing their attention. When they search, copy,
read, or eat, they concentrate on the right; they bump into things on the left, ignore
people approaching from the left, and verbally omit the left of a scene. A second hallmark
is **anosognosia-like unawareness**: patients do not know their egocenter has shifted.

**Why "egocentric," not "visual."** Neglect is defined relative to the **body**, not the
eye or the world. Tilting the patient left/right/forward/back does not move the bias with
gravity (Karnath et al. 1998) — it stays locked to the body. So the disturbance lives in
the **body-centred (egocentric) reference frame**, the internal map that places objects
relative to the trunk (see [[Egocentric Reference Frame and Coordinate Transformation]]).

**Two competing accounts.**
1. **Interhemispheric imbalance (Kinsbourne; Corbetta).** Each hemisphere pulls attention
   to the opposite side; a right lesion leaves the left hemisphere unopposed, producing a
   continuous rightward *gradient* of attention. Prediction: patients should keep turning
   further and further right ("circling").
2. **Egocentric reference-frame account (Karnath).** The top-down control of attention is
   intact; what is broken is the **body-related matrix** on which attention runs — it is
   rotated to a new equilibrium on the right. Prediction: a **bell-shaped** distribution
   of exploration peaking just right of midline and then *decreasing* toward the far right.

The eye-movement data favour account (2): exploration is bell-shaped, not a monotonic
gradient, and the peak sits far from the anatomical limit of rightward gaze. A refinement
distinguishes the two **attention systems**: the **stimulus-driven (bottom-up)** system is
disturbed (delayed/hypometric leftward saccades to sudden targets), while the
**goal-directed (top-down)** system is preserved (voluntary saccades show no
direction-specific deficit within the explored region). This fits the lesion site, which
hits the **ventral** attention network and spares the **dorsal** one.

**Anatomy.** The core deficit maps onto a right **perisylvian network** — see
[[Perisylvian Network for Spatial Orienting]]. Satellite deficits dissociate: allocentric
(object-based) neglect and line-bisection errors sit more posterior/inferior; **extinction**
sits at the TPJ.

**Modulation by sensory input.** Because the egocentric frame is built from multisensory
input, manipulating that input shifts the bias: **caloric vestibular stimulation** and
**posterior neck-muscle vibration** transiently move the exploration centre back toward
the trunk midline and can abolish neglect — and induce neglect-like behaviour in healthy
people. This is the strongest evidence that neglect is a frame-transformation disorder and
points to sensory-stimulation rehabilitation routes.

## Worked example

A clock-drawing test: a neglect patient asked to draw a clock crams all twelve numbers
into the **right half** of the dial, leaving the left half empty — yet, asked, they say the
drawing looks fine. On a cancellation task (cross out all the targets in a cluttered array)
they cancel targets on the right and miss those on the left, and the missed targets are
left-of-the-body, not left-of-each-object necessarily (egocentric core). Under left
cold-caloric stimulation, the same patient now cancels several previously missed left-side
targets — the bias temporarily relaxes.

## Role in this class or project

One of the central spatial disorders of the course. Pairs naturally with
[[Pusher Syndrome]] (the lecture explicitly contrasts them): neglect is a disturbance of
body orientation in the **transverse/yaw plane** (left–right orienting), pusher syndrome in
the **coronal/roll plane** (upright tilt) — separate subsystems for body orientation in
different spatial dimensions.

## Exam, assignment, or project relevance

Core exam material. Be able to: define neglect; describe the at-rest egocentric gaze bias
and unawareness; argue why it is egocentric not gravity/world-based; contrast the

state the top-down-preserved / bottom-up-disturbed dissociation; name the perisylvian
anatomy; explain vestibular/neck-vibration modulation.

## Common confusions

- **Neglect ≠ hemianopia (visual field loss).** A hemianope has a sensory hole and usually
  *knows* it and compensates; a neglect patient has intact vision but does not orient left
  and is unaware. They can co-occur but dissociate.
- **Neglect ≠ a broken top-down attention.** Top-down control is largely preserved; the
  body-centred frame it runs on is shifted.
- **"Subcortical neglect" is usually cortical.** Basal-ganglia/pulvinar lesions cause
  neglect by hypoperfusing intact perisylvian cortex.
- **Core neglect ≠ line-bisection deficit.** ~40% of core-neglect patients bisect lines
  normally; line bisection taps a more posterior, allocentric mechanism.
