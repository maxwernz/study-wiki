# Perisylvian Network for Spatial Orienting

## Short definition

A densely interconnected set of cortical regions straddling the **sylvian fissure** in the
**right** hemisphere — superior/middle temporal cortex (+insula), inferior parietal lobule
/ temporo-parietal junction, and ventrolateral frontal cortex — whose damage produces
**spatial neglect**. The homologous **left** network serves language and praxis instead.

## Intuition

Think of three cities (temporal, parietal, frontal) arranged around a bay (the sylvian
fissure), joined by several highways (white-matter tracts). On the right side of the brain
this network's "job" is keeping track of where your body is in space and orienting you
within it. Damage anywhere in the network — any city, or the highways between them — can
break the same job, which is why neglect can follow lesions in quite different-looking
places. On the left side, the very same city-and-highway layout instead runs language and
skilled action.

## Explanation

**The hubs.** Three grey-matter regions:
- **Superior/middle temporal cortex + insula** (especially the superior temporal gyrus) —
  the region most consistently lesioned in core neglect.
- **Inferior parietal lobule (IPL) / temporo-parietal junction (TPJ)** — TPJ is also the
  key site for **extinction**.
- **Ventrolateral (inferior/middle) frontal cortex.**

**The tracts** connecting them (worth knowing by name):
- IPL ↔ ventrolateral frontal: **superior longitudinal fasciculus II/III (SLF II/III)** and
  **superior occipitofrontal fasciculus (SOF)**.
- ventrolateral frontal ↔ superior/middle temporal: **arcuate fasciculus (AF)** and
  **extreme capsule / inferior occipitofrontal fasciculus (EmC/IOF)**.
- superior temporal ↔ IPL: **middle longitudinal fasciculus (MdLF)** and EmC/IOF.

**Why the network framing matters.** Because the three hubs are tightly linked, lesions at
distant cortical sites around the fissure can all yield the *same* egocentric orienting
deficit. The network's computational role: integrate vestibular, neck-proprioceptive,
auditory and visual signals into the **egocentric representation** of space (see
[[Egocentric Reference Frame and Coordinate Transformation]]) — overlapping the human
multisensory/"vestibular" cortex.

**Grey matter, not just disconnection.** Although the connecting fibres can be involved,
the large majority of the neglect-predictive lesion volume is **grey matter**; pure
white-matter disconnection accounts for only a small fraction. Subcortical (basal-ganglia,
pulvinar) lesions cause neglect mainly by **hypoperfusing** this intact cortex (shown with
perfusion-weighted imaging). So neglect is better seen as a **network/cortical dysfunction**
than a classic disconnection syndrome.

**Lateralization.** The right specialization for spatial orienting parallels the left
specialization for language — a human trait (in monkeys the orienting function is bilateral
and symmetric). This is why right perisylvian damage → neglect, left → aphasia/apraxia.

## Worked example

A patient with a right **basal-ganglia** haemorrhage shows florid neglect, yet the cortex
looks structurally intact on CT. Perfusion imaging reveals **hypoperfusion of the right
superior temporal cortex, IPL and ventrolateral frontal cortex** — the perisylvian hubs.
The neglect is "subcortical" in lesion location but **cortical in mechanism**: the basal-
ganglia stroke starved the perisylvian network of blood.

## Role in this class or project

The anatomy half of the Spatial neglect "What should I know" outline. Also the anatomical
bridge to the **Disconnection syndromes** and **Aphasia/Apraxia** course topics (same
network shape, different hemisphere/function).

## Exam, assignment, or project relevance

Be able to name the three cortical hubs and at least the major tracts (SLF, AF, IOF/EmC,
SOF, MdLF); explain why distant lesions cause the same deficit; explain why subcortical
neglect is cortical (PWI); and state the left/right functional split.

## Common confusions

- The **superior temporal cortex**, not just the parietal lobe, is central to core neglect
  (a correction to older "parietal lobe = neglect" teaching).
- TPJ is specifically tied to **extinction**, which dissociates from core neglect.
