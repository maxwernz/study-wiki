# Pusher Syndrome

## Short definition

A post-stroke disorder of **postural control** in which the patient **actively pushes** with
the non-paretic arm/leg **toward the paralysed (contralesional) side** and **resists** any
attempt to straighten them. The cause is a **misperception of the body's orientation
relative to gravity** in the **coronal (roll) plane**: they feel "upright" when actually
tilted ~20° to the ipsilesional side. Lesion: **posterolateral thalamus** (± insula /
postcentral gyrus); occurs after left or right lesions.

## Intuition

Your inner sense of "I'm sitting up straight" has been miscalibrated: when you are actually
leaning ~20° to one side, it tells you "perfectly upright." So when a nurse straightens you,
it feels to *you* like she's tipping you over — and you push back to "stay upright," which
actually shoves you toward the floor on your weak side. The cruel twist: your **eyes** still
report vertical correctly, so the moment you can see the room, you can line yourself up fine.
The fault is in a **body-based** sense of which way is up, not the visual one.

## Explanation

**The behaviour (contraversive pushing; Davies 1985).** Sitting or standing, the patient
spreads the non-paretic limbs and pushes toward the hemiparetic side; pushed back upright,
they forcefully resist. It is easy to **miss**, because patients are examined lying down —
you must **sit or stand them up** to see it. It affects ~10% of acute hemiparetic strokes and
is usually **transient** (recovers within weeks to ~6 months).

**What it is *not* (differential diagnosis).**
- **Listing phenomenon:** the patient *knows* they are losing balance, falls toward the
  *paretic* side, and clings on — opposite to active contraversive pushing.
- **Lateropulsion** (Wallenberg's brainstem syndrome): falls *ipsiversively*, no active
  pushing or resistance.
- **Thalamic astasia:** similar lesion site but described in patients with little/no
  weakness — may be a dissociable disorder.
- **Not caused by neglect.** It co-occurs with neglect (right lesions) and **aphasia** (left
  lesions) because the cortical pusher area lies near both, but pushing occurs without neglect
  in ~20% of right pushers and in left-lesion pushers who have aphasia, not neglect.

**The mechanism — a second graviceptive system.** Tested with two "vertical" tasks:
- **Subjective Visual Vertical (SVV):** in darkness, set a luminous rod to earth-vertical —
  probes the **visual-vestibular** sense of the *world's* vertical.
- **Subjective Postural Vertical (SPV):** on a tilt chair with eyes closed, say when your
  *body* is earth-vertical — probes the sense of the *body's* upright.

Pushers show a **double dissociation**: **normal SVV** but a **~18–20° ipsilesional tilt of
SPV**. Patients with vestibular disorders show the **opposite** (tilted SVV, normal SPV).
This implies **two separate systems**: the familiar **visual-vestibular** system for the
world's vertical, and a **second, body-based graviceptive system** for the body's upright.

![[outputs/images/pusher-syndrome/svv-spv-dissociation.pdf]]
*Figure — left: the SVV/SPV double dissociation (pushers: normal visual vertical, tilted
postural vertical; vestibular disorders: the reverse). Right: pusher syndrome disturbs body
orientation in the coronal/roll plane, spatial neglect in the transverse/yaw plane.*
Pusher syndrome is a lesion of the **second** one. Candidate inputs: **truncal graviceptors**
(inertia of abdominal viscera/blood; Mittelstaedt) and/or somatosensory signals — though
somatosensory loss **alone** does not cause pushing. A specific sign: the **non-paretic leg
is constantly tilted ipsiversively** (Johannsen et al. 2006).

**A puzzle.** The felt-upright (SPV) is tilted **ipsilesionally**, yet pushing is
**contraversive**. Likely the patient pushes to compensate the mismatch between the (correct)
visual vertical and the (tilted) postural vertical — consistent with the observation that
**pushing eases with eyes closed** — and/or pushing is a reaction to losing balance when they
move toward their ipsilesionally-shifted felt-upright.

**Anatomy.** The **posterolateral thalamus** (VPL/VPM; ventral/lateral posterior nuclei) in
either hemisphere; less often the **insula and postcentral gyrus** (when the thalamus is
spared). These are exactly the relay/cortical targets for graviceptive/somatosensory input —
so they plausibly **process the body-orientation signal**.

![[pusher-lesion-localization.pdf]]
*Figure — where the lesion sits. **A** (axial slice at thalamic level): the **posterolateral
thalamus** (VPL/VPM, red) is the primary site, bilaterally drawn because pushing follows left
*or* right lesions. **B** (lateral view): the **insula** (deep in the Sylvian fissure) and
**postcentral gyrus (S1)** are the secondary cortical sites implicated when the thalamus is
spared (orange). Damaging this body-graviceptive relay/cortex tilts the felt body-upright (SPV)
~18–20° toward the lesion in the coronal (roll) plane while leaving the visual vertical (SVV)
intact.*

**Roll vs yaw — a unifying idea.** Pusher syndrome disturbs body orientation in the
**coronal (roll)** plane; [[Spatial Neglect]] disturbs it in the **transverse (yaw)** plane.
The brain seems to have **separate subsystems for body orientation in different spatial
dimensions** (a putative pitch-plane analogue is camptocormia).

## Worked example

A patient 5 days after a right posterolateral thalamic haemorrhage is sat on the edge of the
bed. He immediately pushes hard with his right (non-paretic) arm, tilting left toward his
paralysed side; the physiotherapist straightens him and he resists. **SVV test (lights off,
rod):** he sets the rod truly vertical — normal. **SPV test (tilt chair, eyes closed):** he
declares himself "upright" when actually tilted ~18° to the right. **Eyes open, viewing the
room:** he aligns his body to the doorframe accurately, and the pushing diminishes. Two weeks
later the pushing has resolved.

## Formal definition / equations

No equation. Quantitative anchors: **SPV ≈ 18–20° ipsilesional tilt**, **SVV ≈ 0° (normal)**;
the **double dissociation** with vestibular disorders (tilted SVV / normal SPV) is the
defining datum.

## Role in this class or project

A "What should I know" topic, and the lecture's explicit foil to [[Spatial Neglect]]
(roll-plane vs yaw-plane body-orientation disturbances; thalamus vs perisylvian cortex).

## Exam, assignment, or project relevance

Be able to: define contraversive pushing; distinguish it from listing/lateropulsion/thalamic
astasia; explain it is not caused by neglect (and the neglect-right / aphasia-left
correlation); explain SVV vs SPV and the double dissociation → second graviceptive system;
name the posterolateral-thalamus lesion; and place pusher (roll) vs neglect (yaw).

## Common confusions

- **Pushing is toward the paralysed/contralesional side** (contraversive) — easy to get
  backwards.
- **SPV (postural, body) is impaired; SVV (visual, world) is normal** — the reverse of
  vestibular disorders.
- **Not caused by neglect** despite the correlation; left-hemisphere pushers have aphasia.
- The felt-upright is tilted **ipsilesionally** even though pushing goes **contralesionally**.
