# Object Orientation Agnosia

## Short definition

A disorder in which a patient can **identify** an object regardless of how it is rotated
but **cannot judge its orientation** (which way is up, which way it points). Identity and
orientation **dissociate**. Typically follows bilateral or right **parietal/temporo-parietal**
lesions.

## Intuition

Imagine being shown an upside-down photo of a familiar politician and instantly saying
"that's the Chancellor" — but if asked "is the picture the right way up?", you genuinely
can't tell. You *know what it is* without *knowing how it sits in space*. That split is the
disorder: the "what" survives, the "which way up" is gone. It shows that the brain can
recognise things without first working out their orientation relative to you.

## Explanation

**The core dissociation.** Patients name letters, objects, animals and faces correctly at
any rotation (0°, ±90°, 180°), yet perform at or near **chance** when asked to report or
correct the orientation of the *non-upright* ones. Some also **copy or write rotated** by
90/180° without noticing. So object **identity** (the "what") is intact while object
**orientation** (a viewer-/space-related property) is lost.

**A refinement: upright is spared.** Careful testing (patient KB; Karnath et al. 2000) shows
**knowledge of the canonical upright is preserved**; only **non-upright** orientation
judgement fails. So it is **not** a *general* agnosia for orientation. This matters
theoretically (below) and is a common exam trap.

**Two theories of object recognition it bears on.**
- **Viewpoint-dependent:** recognise by transforming the image to a stored canonical view —
  find the major axis → rotate to vertical → scale → match a template (the "A" demo). Relies
  on **viewer-centred** information (orientation matters).
- **Viewpoint-invariant:** recognise from an **object-centred** structural description built
  around the object's principal axis (Marr) or from parts/geons (Biederman) — orientation is
  irrelevant.

Object orientation agnosia is taken as evidence that recognition can run on the
**viewpoint-invariant** route **without** orientation information. In the **two-visual-streams**
framing (Milner & Goodale), viewpoint-invariant identity is **ventral** (occipitotemporal),
while viewer-centred orientation is **dorsal/parietal** — so a parietal lesion can knock out
orientation while sparing ventral identity.

**Why upright survives — the tuned-neuron account (Perrett et al. 1998).** Objects are coded
by populations of **neurons tuned to view, orientation and size**. Because we see most things
**upright**, **far more neurons are tuned to upright** views. After damage, the densely-
populated **upright** code is more robust (and recovers earlier) than the sparse non-upright
codes — explaining preserved-upright / lost-non-upright without positing a dedicated
orientation module. This also fits **recognition without mental rotation**: a familiar object
in a non-canonical view is recognised by **evidence accumulation across view-tuned neurons**,
not by rotating an internal image.

## Worked example

Patient KB is shown the letter **A** at four rotations and a silhouette of a **rooster** at
the same rotations. **Identity:** she reads "A" and says "rooster" correctly every time, even
upside down. **Orientation:** asked which card shows the upright version, she reliably picks
the true 0° card (upright spared) but, asked to say how the −90°/+90°/180° cards are rotated,
she answers at chance. Conclusion: identity intact; non-upright orientation lost; upright
knowledge preserved.

## Formal definition / equations

No equation. The conceptual claim: recognition $R$ can be computed from an object-centred
description $D_{\text{obj}}$ that is invariant to the viewing transform $T$:
$R(T \cdot \text{image}) = R(\text{image})$, whereas the **orientation** read-out requires the
viewer-centred term $T$ itself — which is what is lost.

## Common confusions

- **Not a general orientation agnosia:** upright knowledge is preserved; only non-upright
  orientation judgement fails.
- **Not a recognition deficit (agnosia):** identity is intact — that's the whole point.
- **No mental rotation needed:** recognition of misoriented objects uses view-tuned-neuron
  accumulation, not internal rotation (which can dissociate from recognition).
